The Paper that Keeps Showing Up

Let's talk about one of my favorite cryptography papers.

This is the paper that keeps showing up. It's almost comical at this point. It's shown up 5 times in my last semester at EPFL, across 3 different courses. It showed up as part of the paper my colleague and I reviewed for a seminar, it showed up for attribute-based credentials in a course about Privacy-Enhancing-Technologies, it showed up in an advanced Cryptography course on two homeworks, and on the final exam!

Everywhere I go, I see this paper, and no one even seems to be aware of the fact that they're using it!

What is this wonderful gem, you might ask?

Well, it's none other than "Unifying Zero-Knowledge Proofs of Knowledge", by Ueli Maurer 1:

This paper is short, but contains a very interesting result. Maurer taks the humble Schnorr signature / proof, which lets you prove knowledge of a scalar xxx such that x⋅G=Xx \cdot G = Xx⋅G=X, without revealing that scalar, and generalizes this scheme to work for any group homomorphism!

[!note] Note: This is a function φ\varphiφ such that:

φ(a+b)=φ(a)+φ(b)\varphi(a + b) = \varphi(a) + \varphi(b)φ(a+b)=φ(a)+φ(b)

This captures a very wide swathe of functionality, from Schnorr signatures, to Pedersen commitments, to attribute-based credentials, to polynomial commitment schemes. We'll be seeing how to construct all of these examples, and more, in the rest of this post, so let's dive right into it!

Warmup: Schnorr Proofs

As a bit of a warmup, let's go over how Schnorr proofs work. These are also a great example to introduce the relevant security notions we'll be working with, like completeness, soundness, etc.

We'll be working with a cryptographic group G\mathbb{G}G, generated by GGG, of prime order qqq, and with an associated field of scalars Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​.

In a Schnorr proof, the prover has a secret x∈Fqx \in \mathbb{F}_qx∈Fq​, and a public value X∈GX \in \mathbb{G}X∈G. The prover wants to demonstrate that X=x⋅GX = x \cdot GX=x⋅G, without revealing xxx.

For the prover to convince the verifier, the two of them run a protocol together:

P knows x∈FqV knows X∈Gk←RFqK←k⋅G→Kc←RFq←cs←k+c⋅x→ss⋅G=?K+c⋅X\boxed{ \begin{aligned} &\mathcal{P} \text{ knows } x \in \mathbb{F}_q&\quad&\mathcal{V} \text{ knows } X \in \mathbb{G} \cr \cr &k \xleftarrow{R} \mathbb{F}_q\cr &K \gets k \cdot G\cr &&\xrightarrow{K}\cr &&&c \xleftarrow{R} \mathbb{F}_q\cr &&\xleftarrow{c}\cr &s \gets k + c \cdot x\cr &&\xrightarrow{s}\cr &&&s \cdot G \stackrel{?}{=} K + c \cdot X\cr \end{aligned} }​P knows x∈Fq​kR​Fq​K←k⋅Gs←k+c⋅x​K​c​s​​V knows X∈GcR​Fq​s⋅G=?K+c⋅X​​

The prover generates their random nonce kkk, and then sends K:=k⋅GK := k \cdot GK:=k⋅G, which is, in essence, a commitment to this nonce. The verifier responds in turn with a random challenge ccc, which the prover then uses to create their response sss. Finally, the verifier checks that the response is consistent with the challenge and the public input XXX.

Completeness

The first property we want a protocol like this to satisfy is completeness. If xxx is correct, i.e. X=x⋅GX = x \cdot GX=x⋅G, then we need verification to succeed at the end of the protocol, assuming that the prover behaves honestly.

To see why this is the case, we need to use the linearity of scalar multiplication:

(a+b)⋅G=a⋅G+b⋅G(a + b) \cdot G = a \cdot G + b \cdot G(a+b)⋅G=a⋅G+b⋅G

If we apply this to the response sss, we get:

s⋅G=(k+c⋅x)⋅G=k⋅G+c⋅(x⋅G)=K+c⋅Xs \cdot G = (k + c \cdot x) \cdot G = k \cdot G+ c \cdot (x \cdot G) = K + c \cdot Xs⋅G=(k+c⋅x)⋅G=k⋅G+c⋅(x⋅G)=K+c⋅X

And so, the verification check will pass.

It's crucial here that sss is actually equal to k+c⋅xk + c \cdot xk+c⋅x, and that X=x⋅GX = x \cdot GX=x⋅G, which requires the prover to be honest. Completeness just says that if the prover is honest, and that the property they're trying to prove holds, then the protocol will succeed. Completeness doesn't say anything about the case where the prover misbehaves, or where the property doesn't actually hold.

Honest Verifier Zero-Knowledge

One way to demonstrate knowledge of a value xxx such that x⋅G=Xx \cdot G = Xx⋅G=X would be to simply reveal the value xxx. The reason we do this complicated Schnorr protocol instead is because we want to prove that we know such an xxx without revealing it. In fact, we don't want to reveal any information about xxx whatsoever.

This is where the honest verifier zero-knowledge property comes in. In essence, it says that if the verifier behaves honestly, then they don't learn any information about the secret value xxx. At least, not any information that they couldn't have gotten just by knowing the public value XXX.

Capturing the notion of "not learning any information" is tricky. To do this, we use a simulator S\mathcal{S}S. This simulator S\mathcal{S}S takes as input the public input XXX, and the challenge ccc, and then creates values KKK and sss which should have the same distribution of the actual messages sent by the prover in the real protocol.

The intuition here is that because the verifier can't distinguish between the messages from a real protocol, and the messages created by the simulator, then they learn nothing that they didn't know before, since they already had access to both XXX and ccc. It's important that the verifier behave honestly, and choose their challenge ccc at random, and not change the way they choose ccc based on KKK. Because this challenge ccc is independent from KKK, you could generate it before even seeing KKK, which means that the messages that you see after that, including KKK, could be coming from a simulator instead, without the verifier realizing it. On the other hand, if the verifier misbehaves, and chooses ccc differently depending on KKK, then our simulation strategy has to work a bit differently.

In this case, the simulator is pretty simple. We know that the commitment KKK and response sss must satisfy:

s⋅G=K+c⋅Xs \cdot G = K + c \cdot Xs⋅G=K+c⋅X

Our simulator S\mathcal{S}S needs to return (K,s)(K, s)(K,s) as a function of (X,c)(X, c)(X,c). One way to do this is to simply choose sss at random, and then set:

K←s⋅G−c⋅XK \gets s \cdot G - c \cdot XK←s⋅G−c⋅X

Because sss is random, the value KKK ends up also being a random group element, which is the same as for a real execution of the protocol. Ditto with sss itself.

This is enough to show that the Schnorr protocol is honest verifier zero-knowledge.

2-Extractability

At this point, we know that if the prover is honest, then they convince the verifier. We also know that an honest verifier learns nothing by running the protocol. But what if the prover is dishonest?

We don't want the prover to be able to convince the verifier unless they actually know an xxx such that x⋅G=Xx \cdot G = Xx⋅G=X.

Our strategy to show this will be to use an extractor. The idea is that if we have two transcripts (K,c,s)(K, c, s)(K,c,s) and (K,c′,s′)(K, c', s')(K,c′,s′), with c≠c′c \neq c'c=c′, which share the same first message, then our extractor E\mathcal{E}E will be able to produce a value x^\hat{x}x^ satisfying x^⋅G=X{\hat{x} \cdot G = X}x^⋅G=X.

This means that the prover must have known such a value.

The intuition here is that you treat the prover as a machine with the value embedded inside of it, somehow. Then, you can get these two transcripts by rewinding this machine. After sending the first challenge ccc and getting the response sss, you rewind the machine back to the point where it sent KKK, and now you send it a new challenge c′c'c′, and get a new response s′s's′.

Intuitively, if the value xxx is embedded inside of the machine, then rewinding doesn't change this fact. If we can successfully extract the value using these two forked transcripts, that means the value was always present inside of the machine.

Here's another analogy. Let's say we had access to a time machine. We could use this time machine to wind back time to when the prover sent KKK, and use that to get our two transcripts. Now, having a time machine doesn't change whether or not a value was embedded inside of the prover, so this convinces us that they knew this value to begin with.

Note that this rewinding is really more of a theoretical point, which we use in proving the soundness of the protocol. We don't actually need to be able to rewind the protocol. This rewinding might be problematic for concurrent security, but that's a can of worms best left unopened for now.

With that aside, let's actually create this extractor, shall we?

Given our two transcripts (K,c,s)(K, c, s)(K,c,s) and (K,c′,s′)(K, c', s')(K,c′,s′) we know that they must satisfy:

s⋅G=K+c⋅Xs′⋅G=K+c′⋅X\begin{aligned} &s \cdot G = K + c \cdot X\cr &s' \cdot G = K + c' \cdot X\cr \end{aligned}​s⋅G=K+c⋅Xs′⋅G=K+c′⋅X​

If we subtract the two equations, we get:

(s−s′)⋅G=(c−c′)⋅X(s - s') \cdot G = (c - c') \cdot X(s−s′)⋅G=(c−c′)⋅X

Finally, since c≠c′c \neq c'c=c′, we can divide by (c−c′)(c - c')(c−c′) to get:

(s−s′)(c−c′)⋅G=X\frac{(s - s')}{(c - c')} \cdot G = X(c−c′)(s−s′)​⋅G=X

We now see that if our extractor sets:

x^←(s−s′)/(c−c′)\hat{x} \gets (s - s') / (c - c')x^←(s−s′)/(c−c′)

then they've managed to find a value x^\hat{x}x^ such that x^⋅G=X\hat{x} \cdot G = Xx^⋅G=X, as promised.

Aside: Sigma Protocols

This Schnorr protocol is a specific example of what we call a sigma protocol.

This is a three move protocol, wherein the prover sends a commitment, the verifier samples a random public challenge, the prover sends a response, and then a verification check based on the transcript and the public input is run.

The Maurer proofs we'll see in the next section are also sigma protocols.

Because the verifier's challenge is completely public, and the verification check doesn't depend on any internal state in the verifier, we call this a public coin protocol.

What's nice about public coin protocols is that they can be made non-interactive, via the Fiat-Shamir transform. The idea is that instead of having a verifier choose a challenge, we instead emulate this choice with a hash function HHH, whose output should function in the same way as the random challenge.

This allows the prover to create a proof without interacting with anybody else, and this proof can be independently verified by different people.

Schnorr signatures are really just the application of this transform to the protocol we saw earlier, with the inclusion of the message we want to sign, mmm, inside of the hash function, so that the proof is "bound" to this specific message.

Generalization: Maurer Proofs

Maurer proofs 1 can be seen as a vast generalization of Schnorr proofs.

Given a group homomorphism φ:A→B\varphi : \mathbb{A} \to \mathbb{B}φ:A→B, they allow you to prove knowledge of a∈Aa \in \mathbb{A}a∈A such that φ(a)=B\varphi(a) = Bφ(a)=B, without revealing the secret aaa. Specifically, you don't learn anything more about aaa than you could have learned from BBB.

[!note] Note: Given two groups A\mathbb{A}A and B\mathbb{B}B, a homomorphism φ\varphiφ is a function from A\mathbb{A}A to B\mathbb{B}B such that for all a,a′∈Aa, a' \in \mathbb{A}a,a′∈A, we have:

φ(a+a′)=φ(a)+φ(a′)\varphi(a + a') = \varphi(a) + \varphi(a')φ(a+a′)=φ(a)+φ(a′)

Schnorr proofs are a specific case of these proofs, with the homomorphism:

ψ:(Fq,+)→Gψ(x):=x⋅G\begin{aligned} \psi &: (\mathbb{F}_q, +) \to \mathbb{G}\cr \psi(x) &:= x \cdot G\cr \end{aligned}ψψ(x)​:(Fq​,+)→G:=x⋅G​

The protocol for the general case of φ:A→B\varphi : \mathbb{A} \to \mathbb{B}φ:A→B is very similar to Schnorr proofs as well:

P knows a∈AV knows B∈Bk←RAK←φ(k)→Kc←RC←cs←k+c⋅a→sφ(s)=?K+c⋅B\boxed{ \begin{aligned} &\mathcal{P} \text{ knows } a \in \mathbb{A}&\quad&\mathcal{V} \text{ knows } B \in \mathbb{B} \cr \cr &k \xleftarrow{R} \mathbb{A}\cr &K \gets \varphi(k)\cr &&\xrightarrow{K}\cr &&&c \xleftarrow{R} \mathcal{C}\cr &&\xleftarrow{c}\cr &s \gets k + c \cdot a\cr &&\xrightarrow{s}\cr &&&\varphi(s) \stackrel{?}{=} K + c \cdot B\cr \end{aligned} }​P knows a∈AkR​AK←φ(k)s←k+c⋅a​K​c​s​​V knows B∈BcR​Cφ(s)=?K+c⋅B​​

The protocol proceeds in a familar way. The prover generates a random nonce kkk, sends their commitment φ(k)\varphi(k)φ(k), the verifier responds with a challenge ccc, and the prover concludes with their response sss.

The set C\mathcal{C}C denotes the challenge space. The way we define this set depends on the homomorphism φ\varphiφ, and the public value BBB. We'll see what conditions we need this set to satisfy when we prove extraction for this protocol.

Completeness

Completeness holds because φ\varphiφ is a homomorphism:

φ(s)=φ(k+c⋅a)=φ(k)+c⋅φ(a)=K+c⋅B\varphi(s) = \varphi(k + c \cdot a) = \varphi(k) + c \cdot \varphi(a) = K + c \cdot Bφ(s)=φ(k+c⋅a)=φ(k)+c⋅φ(a)=K+c⋅B

Honest Verifier Zero-Knowledge

The simulator is also pretty simple to define.

Given a challenge ccc, we know that KKK and sss must satisfy:

φ(s)=K+c⋅B\varphi(s) = K + c \cdot Bφ(s)=K+c⋅B

The idea behind our simulator, like with Schnorr proofs, is to generate a random sss, and then define KKK as a function of sss, BBB, and ccc:

K←φ(s)−c⋅BK \gets \varphi(s) - c \cdot BK←φ(s)−c⋅B

Since sss is random, this makes KKK as random as in the real protocol, and so our simulator works.

Extraction

Extraction is a bit trickier, because we need to choose our challenge space C\mathcal{C}C. This is simple when we know the order of the group B\mathbb{B}B, but we can also find challenge spaces which work even if the order of the group is unknown.

Our extractor has access to two transcripts (K,c,s)(K, c, s)(K,c,s) and (K,c′,s′)(K, c', s')(K,c′,s′), with c≠c′c \neq c'c=c′. These transcripts are valid, and so satisfy:

φ(s)=K+c⋅Bφ(s′)=K+c′⋅B\begin{aligned} \varphi(s) &= K + c \cdot B\cr \varphi(s') &= K + c' \cdot B\cr \end{aligned}φ(s)φ(s′)​=K+c⋅B=K+c′⋅B​

If we subtract the equations, we get:

φ(s)−φ(s′)=(c−c′)⋅B\varphi(s) - \varphi(s') = (c - c') \cdot Bφ(s)−φ(s′)=(c−c′)⋅B

Since φ(s)\varphi(s)φ(s) is a homomorphism, we can write:

φ(s−s′)=(c−c′)⋅B\varphi(s - s') = (c - c') \cdot Bφ(s−s′)=(c−c′)⋅B

Now, if we knew the order of B\mathbb{B}B, say qqq, then we could invert c−c′c - c'c−c′ mod qqq, giving us:

1(c−c′)⋅φ(s−s′)=B\frac{1}{(c - c')} \cdot \varphi(s - s') = B(c−c′)1​⋅φ(s−s′)=B

and then use the fact that φ(s−s′)\varphi(s - s')φ(s−s′) is a homomorphism, giving us:

φ(s−s′c−c′)=B\varphi \left(\frac{s - s'}{c - c'}\right) = Bφ(c−c′s−s′​)=B

And we could then use this value as our extractor.

But it turns out that you can make the extraction work even if you don't know the order.

To do this, we need to assume the existence of an "anchor value" u∈Au \in \mathbb{A}u∈A, such that there exists lll with:

φ(u)=l⋅B\varphi(u) = l \cdot Bφ(u)=l⋅B

We also require that \gcd(l,(c−c′))=1\gcd(l, (c - c')) = 1gcd(l,(c−c′))=1 for all c≠c′∈Cc \neq c' \in \mathcal{C}c=c′∈C. This means that there exists coefficients α,β∈Z\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{Z}α,β∈Z such that:

α⋅l+β(c−c′)=1\alpha \cdot l + \beta (c - c') = 1α⋅l+β(c−c′)=1

We can then use this to define our extracted value x^\hat{x}x^:

x^:=α⋅u+β⋅(s−s′)\hat{x} := \alpha \cdot u + \beta \cdot (s - s')x^:=α⋅u+β⋅(s−s′)

To see why this works, remember that φ(s−s′)=(c−c′)⋅B\varphi(s - s') = (c - c') \cdot Bφ(s−s′)=(c−c′)⋅B, which means that:

φ(x^)=φ(α⋅u+β⋅(s−s′))=α⋅φ(u)+β⋅φ(s−s′)=α⋅l⋅B+β⋅(c−c′)⋅B=(α⋅l+β⋅(c−c′))⋅B=1⋅B=B\begin{aligned} \varphi(\hat{x}) &=\cr \varphi(\alpha \cdot u + \beta \cdot (s - s')) &=\cr \alpha \cdot \varphi(u) + \beta \cdot \varphi(s - s') &=\cr \alpha \cdot l \cdot B + \beta \cdot (c - c') \cdot B &=\cr (\alpha \cdot l + \beta \cdot (c - c')) \cdot B &=\cr 1 \cdot B &= B\cr \end{aligned}φ(x^)φ(α⋅u+β⋅(s−s′))α⋅φ(u)+β⋅φ(s−s′)α⋅l⋅B+β⋅(c−c′)⋅B(α⋅l+β⋅(c−c′))⋅B1⋅B​======B​

We also need the set C\mathcal{C}C to be large enough, e.g. ∣C∣≥2λ|\mathcal{C}| \geq 2^\lambda∣C∣≥2λ, with λ\lambdaλ our security parameter. Or, we could use a smaller set, and do multiple repetitions of the protocol.

Examples of Extraction

In summary, we need a large set C\mathcal{C}C such that for any c≠c′∈Cc \neq c' \in \mathcal{C}c=c′∈C, there exists an anchor value uuu and exponent lll such that:

φ(u)=l⋅B\varphi(u) = l \cdot Bφ(u)=l⋅B

and \gcd(l,c−c′)=1\gcd(l, c - c') = 1gcd(l,c−c′)=1.

Let's see a few examples of how this works.

Extraction for Groups of Known Prime Order

If you know the order of a group B\mathbb{B}B, and this group has prime order qqq, then you can easily create this set.

The element 0∈A0 \in \mathbb{A}0∈A can act as our anchor value, since q⋅B=0=φ(0)q \cdot B = 0 = \varphi(0)q⋅B=0=φ(0), no matter what value BBB has.

Furthermore, if we take C=Fq\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{F}_qC=Fq​, then the difference between any two elements in Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ will be coprime with qqq, since qqq is a prime number, and the difference will be <q{< q}<q.

In fact, instead of using the order of the group, it suffices to use the exponent. This is a value eee such that e⋅B=0e \cdot B = 0e⋅B=0 for any value B∈BB \in \mathbb{B}B∈B. When taking the product of several groups, this will be smaller than the order, which is nice.

Extraction for RSA

[!note] Note: In this section, and when mentioning RSA later, I will switch to the inferior multiplicative notation, because I fear that the world is not yet ready for additive notation RSA.

Let's say that you want to do this for an RSA group (Z/(N),⋅)(\mathbb{Z}/(N), \cdot)(Z/(N),⋅). The issue is that you don't know the order of this group.

The homomorphism in this case is:

φ(x):=xe\varphi(x) := x^eφ(x):=xe

(done modulo NNN)

You also know a public value yyy, claimed to equal xex^exe for some secret xxx.

This homomorphism naturally guides our choice of anchor. We need to find a uuu such that φ(u)=yl\varphi(u) = y^lφ(u)=yl. Unraveling φ\varphiφ, we need ue=ylu^e = y^lue=yl.

Well, one simple way to satisfy this is to take l=el = el=e and u=yu = yu=y, giving us the equation:

ye=yey^e = y^eye=ye

Now, we need to find a set C\mathcal{C}C where the difference with any two elements is coprime with eee. This is quite simple if eee is a prime number, which is usually the case: we can simply take C=Z/(e)\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{Z}/(e)C=Z/(e). All (non-negative) numbers <e< e<e will have difference which is coprime with eee, since eee is prime.

Now, if eee is a small prime number, then we'll need to repeat our protocol several times for soundness. For example, if eee is 333, then we'd need to repeat our protocol 128/(lg 3)128 / (\text{lg } 3)128/(lg 3) times, for 128128128 bit security.

Summary

In summary, given a group homomorphism φ:A→B\varphi : \mathbb{A} \to \mathbb{B}φ:A→B, Maurer proofs are a sigma protocol for proving knowledge of a secret a∈Aa \in \mathbb{A}a∈A such that φ(a)=B\varphi(a) = Bφ(a)=B, for a public BBB.

The only assumption we need to make about the groups is that we can construct a set C\mathcal{C}C such that there exists an exponent l∈Zl \in \mathbb{Z}l∈Z, with \gcd(l,(c−c′))=1\gcd(l, (c - c')) = 1gcd(l,(c−c′))=1 for all c≠c′∈Cc \neq c' \in \mathcal{C}c=c′∈C, and an anchor u∈Au \in \mathbb{A}u∈A satisfying:

φ(u)=l⋅B\varphi(u) = l \cdot Bφ(u)=l⋅B

Some Applications

We've now seen what Maurer proofs are, and how general they are. While they sure are neat, presented like this they may still be a bit uninteresting. When I first read the paper, I thought it was cool, but nothing to write home about. What really made it one of my favorite papers was accidentally stumbling into a bunch of applications that people weren't even aware were specific instances of Maurer proofs.

In this section, we'll be going over quite a few of these applications.

Schnorr Proofs

I've mentioned this one a few times already, but it's worth seeing again.

With a Schnorr proof, you want to show that you know a secret xxx such that x⋅G=X{x \cdot G = X}x⋅G=X, with XXX public.

We can capture this with a convenient notation for relations:

{(G,X;x) ∣ x⋅G=X}\{(G, X ; x)\ |\ x \cdot G = X\}{(G,X;x) ∣ x⋅G=X}

The elements to the left of the ;;; are public, those to the right are private, and the statement after the ∣|∣ is the relation which needs to hold.

We can write this as a Maurer relation:

{(φ,X;x) ∣ φ(x)=X}\{(\varphi, X ; x)\ |\ \varphi(x) = X\}{(φ,X;x) ∣ φ(x)=X}

With φ(x):=x⋅G\varphi(x) := x \cdot Gφ(x):=x⋅G, which is homomorphic.

The challenge set C\mathcal{C}C can be chosen to be Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​, as explained earlier.

Guillou-Quisquater Proofs

The Guillou-Quisquater protocol 2 is similar to Schnorr proofs, but instead works over an RSA group.

Given an RSA modulus NNN, and public exponent eee (which we assume to be prime), you can define the following relation:

{(N,e,y;x) ∣ xe≡ymodN}\{(N, e, y ; x) \ |\ x^e \equiv y \mod N \}{(N,e,y;x) ∣ xe≡ymodN}

But, this is really just an instance of a Maurer relation, with the homomorphism:

φ:(Z/(N),⋅)→(Z/(N),⋅)φ(x):=xemodN\begin{aligned} &\varphi : (\mathbb{Z}/(N), \cdot) \to (\mathbb{Z}/(N), \cdot)\cr &\varphi(x) := x^e \mod N \end{aligned}​φ:(Z/(N),⋅)→(Z/(N),⋅)φ(x):=xemodN​

We also need to pick our challenge set C\mathcal{C}C. If eee is a prime number, then we can simply take Z/(e)\mathbb{Z}/(e)Z/(e), as we saw earlier.

We might need several parallel repetitions of the protocol if eee is small though.

Parallel Schnorr

One useful extension of Schnorr proofs is when you want to prove multiple instances in parallel:

{(G,A,B;a,b) ∣ a⋅G=A∧b⋅G=B}\{(G, A, B; a, b)\ |\ a \cdot G = A \land b \cdot G = B \}{(G,A,B;a,b) ∣ a⋅G=A∧b⋅G=B}

Interestingly enough, this is also an instance of a Maurer relation, with the homomorphism:

φ:Fq2→G2φ(a,b):=(a⋅G,b⋅G)\begin{aligned} &\varphi : \mathbb{F}^2_q \to \mathbb{G}^2\cr &\varphi(a, b) := (a \cdot G, b \cdot G) \end{aligned}​φ:Fq2​→G2φ(a,b):=(a⋅G,b⋅G)​

For our challenge set, we can simply use Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​, since lcm(q,q)=q\text{lcm}(q, q) = qlcm(q,q)=q.

In general, taking products of group homomorphisms is an extremely useful way to create Maurer relations.

Pedersen Commitment Proofs

A Pedersen commitment requires two independent generators G,H∈GG, H \in \mathbb{G}G,H∈G, and forms the commitment to xxx as:

x⋅G+r⋅Hx \cdot G + r \cdot Hx⋅G+r⋅H

for some blinding factor r∈Fqr \in \mathbb{F}_qr∈Fq​.

One useful relation is proving that a value X=x⋅GX = x \cdot GX=x⋅G, with xxx hidden by a commitment CCC:

{(G,H,X,C;x,r) ∣ x⋅G=X∧x⋅G+r⋅H=C}\{(G, H, X, C; x, r)\ |\ x \cdot G = X \land x \cdot G + r \cdot H = C \}{(G,H,X,C;x,r) ∣ x⋅G=X∧x⋅G+r⋅H=C}

As you might guess, this is in fact a Maurer relation, for the homomorphism:

φ:Fq2→G2φ(x,r):=(x⋅G,x⋅G+r⋅H)\begin{aligned} &\varphi : \mathbb{F}_q^2 \to \mathbb{G}^2 \cr &\varphi(x, r) := (x \cdot G, x \cdot G + r \cdot H) \end{aligned}​φ:Fq2​→G2φ(x,r):=(x⋅G,x⋅G+r⋅H)​

Our challenge set can be Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ once again.

We can also have an arbitrary homomorphism, and not just x⋅Gx \cdot Gx⋅G, which is thanks to the flexibility of Maurer proofs.

Oblivious PRFs

Another interesting example comes from the Privacy Pass paper 3.

In essence, a server knows a secret key kkk that allows them to calculate a prf via:

P↦k⋅PP \mapsto k \cdot PP↦k⋅P

If you present (P,Q)(P, Q)(P,Q) such that Q=k⋅PQ = k \cdot PQ=k⋅P, then the server is convinced that it calculated that pair for you, since only it knows kkk, and inverting the discrete logarithm is hard. In this paper, they use this to create redeemable tokens as an alternative to completing captchas.

They also want these tokens to be unlinkable, so they describe a protocol which allows getting such a pair (P,Q)(P, Q)(P,Q) without revealing it to the server.

The idea is to send P′:=r⋅PP' := r \cdot PP′:=r⋅P, have the server reply with k⋅P′k \cdot P'k⋅P′, which you can then unblind to get Q:=r−1⋅k⋅P′=k⋅PQ := r^{-1} \cdot k \cdot P' = k \cdot PQ:=r−1⋅k⋅P′=k⋅P.

One subtlety is that you need to make sure that the server actually used the same kkk here that they've been using for other people.

One way to do this is for the server to publish K:=k⋅GK := k \cdot GK:=k⋅G in advance, and then provide a proof for the relation:

{(G,P′,Q′;k) ∣ k⋅G=K∧k⋅P′=Q′}\{(G, P', Q' ; k )\ |\ k \cdot G = K \land k \cdot P' = Q' \}{(G,P′,Q′;k) ∣ k⋅G=K∧k⋅P′=Q′}

And, if you can believe it, this is an instance of a Maurer relation, with the homomorphism:

φ(k):=(k⋅G,k⋅P′)\varphi(k) := (k \cdot G, k \cdot P')φ(k):=(k⋅G,k⋅P′)

Attribute-Based Credentials

Attribute-based credentials are an amazing piece of technology which I won't have time to explain here. I will only use a little bit of the Pointcheval-Sanders scheme 4 here anyways.

Their scheme uses two proofs.

The first is for the relation:

{(G,C,Y1,…,Yn;t,a1,…,an) ∣ C=t⋅G+∑iai⋅Yi}\{(G, C, Y_1, \ldots, Y_n; t, a_1, \ldots, a_n) \ |\ C = t \cdot G + \sum_i a_i \cdot Y_i \}{(G,C,Y1​,…,Yn​;t,a1​,…,an​) ∣ C=t⋅G+i∑​ai​⋅Yi​}

This is, as you might have come to expect by now, an instance of a Maurer relation, with the homomorphism:

φ(t,a1,…,an):=t⋅G+∑iai⋅Yi\varphi(t, a_1, \ldots, a_n) := t \cdot G + \sum_i a_i \cdot Y_iφ(t,a1​,…,an​):=t⋅G+i∑​ai​⋅Yi​

The second proof they use is for the substantially more complicated relation:

{(σ1,σ2,G~,X~,Y~1,…,Y~n;t,a1,…,an) ∣ e(σ2,G~)−e(σ1,X~)=t⋅e(σ1,G~)+∑iai⋅e(σ1,Y~i)}\begin{aligned} &\{(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \tilde{G}, \tilde{X}, \tilde{Y}_1, \ldots, \tilde{Y}_n ; t, a_1, \ldots, a_n) \ | \ \cr &e(\sigma_2, \tilde{G}) - e(\sigma_1, \tilde{X}) = t \cdot e(\sigma_1, \tilde{G}) + \sum_i a_i \cdot e(\sigma_1, \tilde{Y}_i)\cr &\} \end{aligned}​{(σ1​,σ2​,G~,X~,Y~1​,…,Y~n​;t,a1​,…,an​) ∣ e(σ2​,G~)−e(σ1​,X~)=t⋅e(σ1​,G~)+i∑​ai​⋅e(σ1​,Y~i​)}​

(Here eee denotes a pairing G1×G2→GT\mathbb{G}_1 \times \mathbb{G}_2 \to \mathbb{G}_TG1​×G2​→GT​)

I had to implement this for a class, and the first time I saw this, I was a bit taken aback.

That is, until I realized that this was an instance of a Maurer relation.

Indeed, all of the pairing stuff is really fluff, because those just become public values. If we squint a bit, we have:

■=t⋅■+∑iai⋅■\blacksquare = t \cdot \blacksquare + \sum_i a_i \cdot \blacksquare■=t⋅■+i∑​ai​⋅■

But this part on the right is just a homomorphism of ttt and aia_iai​, which we can write as:

φ(t,a1,…,an):=t⋅e(σ1,G~)+∑iai⋅e(σ1,Y~i)\varphi(t, a_1, \ldots, a_n) := t \cdot e(\sigma_1, \tilde{G}) + \sum_i a_i \cdot e(\sigma_1, \tilde{Y}_i)φ(t,a1​,…,an​):=t⋅e(σ1​,G~)+i∑​ai​⋅e(σ1​,Y~i​)

The output of this group is an element in GT\mathbb{G}_TGT​, so it suffices to take integers less than the smallest prime factor in the order of GT\mathbb{G}_TGT​ as our challenge set.

TWW Polynomial Commitment Scheme

Finally, let's conclude our examples with a polynomial commitment scheme, which doesn't require any fancing pairings or trusted setups.

A polynomial ppp of degree ddd consists of d+1d + 1d+1 coefficients:

p0+p1⋅X+…+pd⋅Xdp_0 + p_1 \cdot X + \ldots + p_{d} \cdot X^{d}p0​+p1​⋅X+…+pd​⋅Xd

Rather than an element of F_q[X]_≤d\mathbb{F}\_q[X]\_{\leq d}F_q[X]_≤d, we can see this polynomial as a vector p∈Fqd+1{\bold{p} \in \mathbb{F}_q^{d + 1}}p∈Fqd+1​.

If we have d+2d + 2d+2 independent generators H,G0,…,GdH, G_0, \ldots, G_dH,G0​,…,Gd​, we can create a commitment scheme in the same way as for Pedersen commitments:

Com(p,r):=r⋅H+∑ipi⋅Gi\text{Com}(p, r) := r \cdot H + \sum_i p_i \cdot G_iCom(p,r):=r⋅H+i∑​pi​⋅Gi​

One neat property of this scheme is that it's homomorphic with respect to its inputs ppp and rrr.

This is all fine and dandy, but not very useful unless we can create opening proofs.

This is a proof that:

{(y,x,C;p,r) ∣ Com(p,r)=C∧p(x)=y}\{(y, x, C; p, r)\ |\ \text{Com}(p, r) = C \land p(x) = y\}{(y,x,C;p,r) ∣ Com(p,r)=C∧p(x)=y}

In other words, the polynomial behind the commitment evaluates to yyy at the point xxx.

Now, the Com(p,r)\text{Com}(p, r)Com(p,r) part is homomorphic, but what about the p(x)=yp(x) = yp(x)=y part?

Well, one way to write p(x)p(x)p(x) is as:

∑ipi⋅xi\sum_i p_i \cdot x^ii∑​pi​⋅xi

From here, it's clear that p(x)+q(x)=(p+q)(x)p(x) + q(x) = (p + q)(x)p(x)+q(x)=(p+q)(x):

(∑ipi⋅xi)+(∑iqi⋅xi)=∑i(pi+qi)⋅xi(\sum_i p_i \cdot x^i) + (\sum_i q_i \cdot x^i) = \sum_i (p_i + q_i) \cdot x^i(i∑​pi​⋅xi)+(i∑​qi​⋅xi)=i∑​(pi​+qi​)⋅xi

Because of this, our opening proof is merely an instance of a Maurer relation!

The homomorphism we use is:

φ(p,r):=(r⋅H+∑ipi⋅Gi,∑ipi⋅xi)\varphi(p, r) := (r \cdot H + \sum_i p_i \cdot G_i, \sum_i p_i \cdot x^i)φ(p,r):=(r⋅H+i∑​pi​⋅Gi​,i∑​pi​⋅xi)

And we can use Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ as our challenge set, once again.

The disadvantage of this scheme is that opening proofs are quite large.

The communication complexity is ∣G∣+(d+2)⋅∣Fq∣|\mathbb{G}| + (d + 2) \cdot |\mathbb{F}_q|∣G∣+(d+2)⋅∣Fq​∣, because we need to send a group element and a scalar element as our first message (for the output of φ\varphiφ), and then we need to send our response, which consists of one scalar for each coefficient in our polynomial.

These disadvantages are why I call this the world's worst polynomial commitment scheme.

Conclusion

Hopefully I could impart to you some of the awe I've come to have for Maurer's paper. It's really a very neat gem with a surprising number of use cases. It's quite the shame that more people aren't aware of it!

We've seen how to use it for oblivious PRF proofs, for attribute-based credentials, for polynomial commitments, and of course, for the humble Schnorr signature.

I think you'll be able to find many more use-cases which I haven't mentioned in this post, such as final exams in cryptography courses :).

The paper itself is a pretty easy read, so you might want to check that out as well, along with the other references.
