Using Maurer Proofs

c.f. my blog post on Maurer proofs

As a starting point, consider the following toy relation:

{(A,B,G;a,b) ∣cra⋅G=Ab⋅G=Bb=a2}\begin{aligned} &\{(A, B, G; a, b)\ |\\cr &\quad a \cdot G = A\cr &\quad b \cdot G = B\cr &\quad b = a^2\cr &\}\cr \end{aligned}cr​{(A,B,G;a,b) ∣a⋅G=Ab⋅G=Bb=a2}​

The tricky aspect is the b=a2b = a^2b=a2 part. The rest can be done with a Maurer proof.

One way around this is to tweak the relation slightly:

{(A,B,G;a,b) ∣cra⋅G=Aa⋅A=Bb⋅G=B}\begin{aligned} &\{(A, B, G; a, b)\ |\\cr &\quad a \cdot G = A\cr &\quad a \cdot A = B\cr &\quad b \cdot G = B\cr &\}\cr \end{aligned}cr​{(A,B,G;a,b) ∣a⋅G=Aa⋅A=Bb⋅G=B}​

Because b=a2b = a^2b=a2, we have a⋅A=a2⋅G=b⋅G=Ba \cdot A = a^2 \cdot G = b \cdot G = Ba⋅A=a2⋅G=b⋅G=B, so this relation is equivalent. Notice also that this relation is captured by the homomorphism:

φ(a,b):=(a⋅G,a⋅A,b⋅G)\varphi(a, b) := (a \cdot G, a \cdot A, b \cdot G)φ(a,b):=(a⋅G,a⋅A,b⋅G)

A Maurer proof will work, wherein you check that φ(a,b)=(A,B,B)\varphi(a, b) = (A, B, B)φ(a,b)=(A,B,B)

We can extend this to the degree 333 case as well:

{(A,B,C,G;a,b,c) ∣cra⋅G=Ab⋅G=Bc⋅G=Cb=a2c=a3}\begin{aligned} &\{(A, B, C, G; a, b, c)\ |\\cr &\quad a \cdot G = A\cr &\quad b \cdot G = B\cr &\quad c \cdot G = C\cr &\quad b = a^2\cr &\quad c = a^3\cr &\}\cr \end{aligned}cr​{(A,B,C,G;a,b,c) ∣a⋅G=Ab⋅G=Bc⋅G=Cb=a2c=a3}​

this time, we use the homomorphism:

φ(a,b,c):=(a⋅G,a⋅A,b⋅G,a⋅B,c⋅G)\varphi(a, b, c) := (a \cdot G, a \cdot A, b \cdot G, a \cdot B, c \cdot G)φ(a,b,c):=(a⋅G,a⋅A,b⋅G,a⋅B,c⋅G)

with expected output (A,B,B,C,C)(A, B, B, C, C)(A,B,B,C,C).

Tau Proof

The relation you want to prove is:

{(Pi,j,Pi,j−1;r) ∣ ri⋅Pi,j−1=Pi,j}\{(P_{i, j}, P_{i, j - 1}; r)\ |\ r^i \cdot P_{i, j - 1} = P_{i, j}\}{(Pi,j​,Pi,j−1​;r) ∣ ri⋅Pi,j−1​=Pi,j​}

(taking the convention τ+=τ\tau^+ = \tauτ+=τ)

If you had a vector r\bold{r}r such that ri=ri\bold{r}_i = r^iri​=ri, then you could just use a standard Maurer proof here. The issue with that is that you need to enforce a specific relation between the vector elements.

We can use the proof we developed in the previous section to get around this restriction though.

We publish R:=r⋅G\bold{R} := \bold{r} \cdot GR:=r⋅G, and then create a proof for the following relation:

{(Pi,j,Pi,j−1,R;r) ∣cr∀i∈[n−1]. r1⋅Ri=Ri+1∀i∈[n]. ri⋅G=Ri∀i∈[n]. ri⋅Pi,j−1=Pi,j}\begin{aligned} &\{(P_{i, j}, P_{i, j - 1}, \bold{R}; \bold{r})\ |\\cr &\quad \forall i \in [n - 1].\ \bold{r}_1 \cdot \bold{R}_i = \bold{R}_{i + 1}\cr &\quad \forall i \in [n].\ \bold{r}_i \cdot G = \bold{R}_{i}\cr &\quad \forall i \in [n].\ \bold{r}_i \cdot P_{i, j - 1} = P_{i, j}\cr &\}\cr \end{aligned}cr​{(Pi,j​,Pi,j−1​,R;r) ∣∀i∈[n−1]. r1​⋅Ri​=Ri+1​∀i∈[n]. ri​⋅G=Ri​∀i∈[n]. ri​⋅Pi,j−1​=Pi,j​}​

This can be done via a Maurer proof for the following homomorphism:

φ(r):=([r_i⋅R_i],[r_i⋅P_i,j−1])\varphi(\bold{r}) := ([\bold{r}\_i \cdot \bold{R}\_i], [\bold{r}\_i \cdot P\_{i, j - 1}])φ(r):=([r_i⋅R_i],[r_i⋅P_i,j−1])

With the expected output being:

(R_i≥2,[P_i,j])(\bold{R}\_{i \geq 2}, [P\_{i, j}])(R_i≥2,[P_i,j])

The vector r\bold{r}r has to be initialized to the powers of rrr.
