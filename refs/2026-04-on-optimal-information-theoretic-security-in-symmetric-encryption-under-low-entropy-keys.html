Abstract

We study the achievable level of information-theoretic security for symmetric encryption under low-entropy keys (e.g., passwords and biometrics), where classical notions such as perfect secrecy and entropic security are usually unattainable. We consider a model in which messages MMM and keys KKK are drawn independently from distributions (pm,pk)(p_\mathrm{m}, p_\mathrm{k})(pm​,pk​). Prior work on homophonic ciphers (HC) and honey encryption (HE) suggests that randomized encryption tailored to pmp_\mathrm{m}pm​ can improve security. We ask what the optimal achievable level is among all symmetric encryption schemes, and which necessary and/or sufficient conditions on encryption schemes characterize when this level can be achieved.

For key confidentiality (KC), we show that the optimal achievable level is I(K;C)=negl(∣C∣)I(K;C)=\mathsf{negl}(|C|)I(K;C)=negl(∣C∣), i.e., the ciphertext reveals only negligible information about the key. Moreover, this holds if and only if, informally, decrypting under any key induces sampling of messages according to pmp_\mathrm{m}pm​. HC and HE following this principle achieve this level, whereas pmp_\mathrm{m}pm​-agnostic schemes do not in general. For message confidentiality (MC) against message-recovery attacks, we show that the optimal bound on the adversary’s success probability is p\max+negl(∣C∣)p_{\max}+\mathsf{negl}(|C|)pmax​+negl(∣C∣), where p\maxp_{\max}pmax​ is the baseline success probability of guessing the most likely message or key under (pm,pk)(p_\mathrm{m}, p_\mathrm{k})(pm​,pk​). We construct a scheme OE\mathsf{OE}OE tailored to (pm,pk)(p_\mathrm{m}, p_\mathrm{k})(pm​,pk​) that attains p\max+O(2−∣C∣)p_{\max}+O(2^{-|C|})pmax​+O(2−∣C∣), and prove that pkp_\mathrm{k}pk​-agnostic schemes (including HC and HE) cannot in general achieve this bound. Our results on necessary and/or sufficient conditions characterize fundamental principles governing the use of randomness in probabilistic encryption.

The main technical challenge is to handle a discretization-induced negligible error that must be propagated throughout the derivation of our bounds and necessary and/or sufficient conditions. To facilitate the analysis, we introduce a continuous-ciphertext framework that separates structural constraints from discretization error.
