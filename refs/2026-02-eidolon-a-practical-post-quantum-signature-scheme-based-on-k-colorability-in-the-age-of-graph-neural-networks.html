Abstract

We propose Eidolon, a practical post-quantum signature scheme grounded in the NP-complete kkk-colorability problem. Our construction generalizes the Goldreich–Micali–Wigderson zero-knowledge protocol to arbitrary k≥3k \geq 3k≥3, applies the Fiat–Shamir transform, and uses Merkle-tree commitments to compress signatures from O(tn)O(tn)O(tn) to O(t\logn)O(t \log n)O(tlogn). Crucially, we generate hard instances via planted “quiet” colorings that preserve the statistical profile of random graphs. We present the first empirical security analysis of such a scheme against both classical solvers (ILP, DSatur) and a custom graph neural network (GNN) attacker. Experiments show that for n≥60n \geq 60n≥60, neither approach recovers the secret coloring, demonstrating that well-engineered kkk-coloring instances can resist modern cryptanalysis, including machine learning. This revives combinatorial hardness as a credible foundation for post-quantum signatures.
