(2026-05) On the Communication Complexity of Sleepy Consensus

  • [[Qiang Tang]]
  • [[Yuchen Ye]]
2026-05-15
  • #cryptography

Abstract

Sleepy consensus allows parties to join and leave execution arbitrarily, which is a fundamental requirement for large-scale distributed systems. Classic longest-chain protocols, such as Bitcoin and its variants, achieve consensus under this model but suffer from inherent long latency. In contrast, recent protocols that build upon the classic view-based BFT paradigm can achieve constant expected latency and short best-case latency under optimal resilience, but they often incur high communication cost. We observe that the high communication overhead stems from the time-shifted quorums, a technique that makes quorum certificates transferable under dynamic participation. However, the technique relies on extensive message forwarding to reconcile parties' inconsistent local views, and thus incurs a cubic communication cost unavoidably.

In this work, we tackle the problem by proposing a novel way to transfer certificates. Building on this, we construct a Byzantine Agreement (BA) protocol secure against the state-of-the-art growing adversary model. Our BA protocol achieves optimal resilience, constant expected round complexity, and an expected communication complexity of O(nNL+nNκ+nN\logN)O(nNL+nN\kappa+nN\log N)O(nNL+nNκ+nNlogN), where nnn is the maximum number of awake parties throughout the execution, NNN is the total number of eligible parties, LLL is the input length, and κ\kappaκ is the security parameter. We also present an efficient recovery mechanism for our BA, incurring only O(Nκ+nL)O(N\kappa+nL)O(Nκ+nL) bits per recovering party. Then we extend our BA to an Atomic Broadcast (ABC) protocol that achieves optimal resilience, constant expected latency, and an expected amortized communication complexity of O(nNL+nNκ+nN\logN)O(nNL+nN\kappa+nN\log N)O(nNL+nNκ+nNlogN) per input value. The recovery mechanism for our ABC incurs O(Nκ+nℓL+nℓκ)O(N\kappa+n\ell L+n\ell \kappa)O(Nκ+nℓL+nℓκ) bits per recovering party, where ℓ\ellℓ is the number of views that the party has slept for. Last but not least, we establish communication lower bounds of Ω(N2L)\Omega(N^2L)Ω(N2L) for sleepy BA and ABC. The result shows that our BA and ABC are communication-optimal when LLL is sufficiently large (i.e., when L=Ω(κ+\logN)L=\Omega(\kappa+\log N)L=Ω(κ+logN)), and highlights a fundamental limitation of communication efficiency in the sleepy model.
