Abstract

The sum-check protocol underpins SNARKs with the fastest known provers. For an nnn-variate polynomial ggg defined over a finite field F\mathbb{F}F, the protocol enables an untrusted prover to convince a verifier of the sum of all evaluations of ggg over a product set HnH^nHn with H⊂FH \subset \mathbb{F}H⊂F. The standard choice for HnH^nHn is the Boolean hypercube {0,1}n\{0,1\}^n{0,1}n, which serves as a natural interpolating set for multilinear polynomials.

We propose a projective variant of the sum-check protocol, obtained by changing the interpolating set from {0,1}n\{0,1\}^n{0,1}n to the infinity hypercube {0,∞}n\{0,\infty\}^n{0,∞}n. Under a suitable notion of evaluation at ∞\infty∞, evaluating a multilinear polynomial at a point in {0,∞}n\{0,\infty\}^n{0,∞}n directly extracts its corresponding monomial coefficient.

This projective viewpoint is a near-drop-in replacement for applications of sum-check, requiring only local changes to polynomial representations, round identities, and evaluation formulas. It yields a ≈10%{\approx}\,10\%≈10% end-to-end speedup for the sum-check prover on BN254 and on a pseudo-Mersenne 128-bit prime field, against a fair baseline. It eliminates all field subtractions when binding a multilinear polynomial, and for structured polynomials such as equality and less-than, the projective interpolants admit evaluation procedures with fewer field operations. Moreover, the monomial-coefficient form aligns naturally with polynomial commitment schemes like WHIR, removing a basis mismatch that these schemes otherwise need to work around.

Finally, we describe an optimization for sum-check over ≈256\approx 256≈256-bit prime fields. When targeting ≈128\approx 128≈128 bits of security, it suffices to sample challenges from a subset of size ≈2128\approx 2^{128}≈2128. We show that a suitable choice of this subset, interpreted as upper-limb values in Montgomery form, yields a 1.92×1.92\times1.92× speedup for field multiplication. Combined with the projective binding formula, this gives a 1.82×1.82\times1.82× speedup for sum-check binding (a key component of fast sum-check proving).
