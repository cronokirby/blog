Abstract

In recent years, the shuffle model has emerged as a prevalent paradigm in privacy-preserving data analysis, centered on the principle of \textit{privacy amplification via shuffling}: an individual user's report is obscured by the ``background noise'' of other users' messages, a phenomenon intuitively known as the \textit{privacy blanket}. This paper initiates a foundational and systematic study of this mechanism from an information-theoretic perspective.
