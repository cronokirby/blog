Universal Properties and Adjunctions

"Free" constructions are abundant in Algebra, and are actually examples of Adjunctions. Specifically, we have an adjunction:

Set⇄?FAlg\bold{Set} \xtofrom[?]{F} \bold{Alg}SetF?​Alg

Where F:Set→AlgF : \bold{Set} \to \bold{Alg}F:Set→Alg is the free functor from the category of sets to this category of algebraic structures, and ?:Alg→Set? : \bold{Alg} \to \bold{Set}?:Alg→Set is the forgetful functor, which forgets the additional algebraic structure.

Usually, this universal construction is presented a bit differently, but is equivalent to the notion of adjunction.

Conclusion

Given functors L:C→DL : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}L:C→D and R:D→CR : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}R:D→C, these statements are equivalent:

  1. L⊣RL \dashv RL⊣R
  2. D(L−,−)≅C(−,R−)\mathcal{D}(L -, -) \cong \mathcal{C}(-, R -)D(L−,−)≅C(−,R−)
